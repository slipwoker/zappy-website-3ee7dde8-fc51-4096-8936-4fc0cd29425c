    Navigating Regulation in Complex Design Changes: Mission Impossible?

    üìÖ November 22, 2023‚è±Ô∏è Reading time: 6 minutes

    Obtaining initial regulatory approval (such as 510(k) or CE approval) is a tremendous achievement for any medical device company. However, the real challenge often begins after that, when the need arises to make changes to the product. These changes can stem from various reasons: supplier changes, component obsolescence, performance improvements, or market feedback. As an expert in obtaining regulatory approvals for design changes in the medical device field, I frequently encounter companies' fear of making necessary changes due to concern about losing existing approval.

    The Dilemma: Design Change vs. Regulatory Stability

    In this case, we worked with a manufacturer of infusion pumps. One of the critical electronic components in the pump went End of Life, and the company was forced to find an alternative. The available alternative required a change to the printed circuit board (PCB) and a minor software update. The concern was that the change would be considered a 'substantial change' requiring resubmission to the FDA, an expensive and lengthy process. This is where expertise in understanding the nuances of regulation comes into play. Not every change requires resubmission, but every change requires documentation and proof that safety and effectiveness have not been compromised.

    Assessment and Documentation Process

    We led a structured change assessment process (Letter to File). The process included comprehensive risk analysis to ensure the new component does not present new risks or change the existing risk profile. We conducted comprehensive verification testing to compare performance between the old and new components. Leading the implementation of PLM documentation and change management systems was critical in this case, as it allowed us to accurately track the impact of the change on all device subsystems and ensure all relevant documents were updated.

    The file we prepared included clear engineering justification, comparative test results, and regulatory analysis based on FDA guidance for deciding when to submit a new 510(k) (Deciding When to Submit a 510(k) for a Change to an Existing Device). The reasoned conclusion was that the change does not require a new submission, but only internal documentation.

    Regulatory and Business Security

    Thanks to professional guidance, the company was able to continue manufacturing and marketing the pumps without interruption, saving tens of thousands of dollars that would have been invested in an unnecessary submission, and more importantly ‚Äì preventing production line stoppage. The case highlights the importance of a regulatory expert who also understands the engineering and operational aspects. The ability to bridge between technical requirements and legal requirements is the key to managing healthy and long-term product life in our regulated industry.
